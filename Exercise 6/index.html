Data Story

The TV Energy Story

A narrative journey through Australia's TV energy consumption data — told for everyday households making purchasing decisions.

Chapter 01

Who is this for?

This story is written for Australian households — renters, homeowners, and families who are buying or replacing a TV and want to make a smarter, more energy-conscious decision. They are not technical experts; they understand star ratings exist but may not realise how relative those ratings are.

Their key question is simple: "Which TV should I buy if I care about my electricity bill?"

Audience priorities: Running cost over time · Easy-to-understand comparisons · Practical size guidance · Technology trade-offs
Chapter 02

Not all screens are born equal

Australia's appliance market features several dominant screen technologies: LED remains the most common, found across budget to mid-range sets. OLED panels light each pixel individually, switching off completely in dark scenes — a natural energy advantage. QLED enhances LED backlighting with quantum dots, offering brightness with moderate efficiency. Plasma, now discontinued, was historically the hungriest technology.

LED dominates by sheer volume — it's what most Australians will encounter when shopping. But OLED's efficiency story is compelling for larger screens.

Screen Technology — Market Frequency
Chapter 03

Size is the single biggest driver of power use

The relationship between screen size and power consumption is close to linear: larger screens need more backlight, more processing power, and larger circuits. A 32-inch set draws around 40W; step up to 75 inches and you're looking at 165W — four times the draw. At typical Australian electricity prices (~$0.30/kWh), that gap translates to roughly $35–$45 extra per year just from screen size alone.

The chart on the Data & Charts page shows this relationship interactively — hover over each bar to see the exact wattage for each size category.

Rule of thumb: Every 10-inch increase in screen size adds approximately 25–35W to your TV's power draw.
Chapter 04

Brand diversity — more models, more choice

Several brands dominate the Australian market in terms of model variety. Samsung and LG offer the widest catalogue, giving consumers the most choice. Sony focuses on fewer, premium lines. Smaller brands like TCL and Hisense have grown rapidly with budget-efficient options that still carry competitive star ratings.

More models doesn't automatically mean better energy options — but it does mean consumers have more room to comparison-shop within their preferred brand.

Chapter 05

Which technology sips the least power?

When we compare average power consumption per screen technology, OLED emerges as the most efficient at comparable screen sizes — thanks to its ability to turn off individual pixels rather than maintaining a full backlight. Standard LED performs well at smaller sizes but becomes less competitive at 65"+ where the backlight demands spike.

QLED sits in the middle — brighter than OLED in some scenarios but drawing more consistent baseline power.

Chapter 06

Star ratings: helpful, but read the fine print

Australia's energy star rating system rates TVs relative to other TVs of the same size. This is crucial to understand: a 5-star 75″ television may still consume 180W — far more than a 2-star 32″ set drawing 35W. The rating tells you how efficient a TV is for its size, not how much electricity it will actually use.

The data shows a mild inverse relationship: as screen size grows, achieving high star ratings becomes harder, and average star ratings slightly decline. Consumers shopping purely by stars, without checking wattage, may end up with a surprising electricity bill.

⚠️ Watch out: A 5-star 75″ TV can cost 4× more to run than a 3-star 32″ TV. Always check the annual energy consumption figure on the label (kWh/year), not just the stars.
Conclusion

The smarter buying decision

For Australians choosing a new TV, the data points to a clear framework: buy the smallest screen that comfortably fits your space, choose OLED if budget allows for large screens, check the kWh/year figure on the energy label (not just the stars), and favour brands with a wide model range so you can comparison-shop within your budget.

The difference between a thoughtful and an impulsive TV purchase could mean $50–$100 saved every year on electricity — over a TV's 7–10 year lifespan, that's real money back in your pocket.

5 Ways to Cut Your TV's Energy Use

01

Match size to room

Choose the smallest screen that looks great from your typical viewing distance. Bigger isn't always better for your eyes or wallet.

02

Check kWh, not just stars

The energy label shows annual kWh consumption — compare this number directly between models rather than star ratings alone.

03

Use Eco mode

Most modern TVs include an Eco or Energy Saving mode that reduces brightness and can cut consumption by 20–40%.

04

Turn off standby

Standby power draws 1–5W constantly. Using the switch at the wall when not in use adds up to a meaningful annual saving.

05

Consider OLED at 55″+

At larger sizes, OLED's per-pixel lighting offers the best balance of picture quality and energy efficiency available today.

About this project

About & Data Sources

About the Data

Source: Australian Government's Equipment Energy Efficiency (E3) Program appliance register — a publicly available dataset of energy-rated televisions sold in Australia.

Data Processing: Data was explored in KNIME, grouped by screen size category and technology type, and average mode power (watts) was calculated per group for visualisation.

Privacy: The dataset contains no personal information. All records are product specifications submitted by manufacturers for public energy rating registration.

Accuracy & Limitations: Power figures represent manufacturer-declared mode power under test conditions — real-world usage may vary with brightness settings, content type, and ambient light. Older or discontinued models may be included in averages.

Ethics: No data has been manipulated to favour particular brands or technologies. Comparisons are presented across full dataset averages to minimise selection bias.
